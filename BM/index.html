The project

Crowd-based methodologies
to enable participatory archaeological research.

The MicroPasts project is carried out by the British Museum in collaboration with the University College London (UCL) and the Institute of Archaeology, supported by the AHRC ‘Digital Transformations in Community Research Co-Production in the Arts and Humanities’ fund.

It aims to explore the use of crowd-based methodologies to enable participatory archaeological research.

Scifabric's work lead to the creation of more than 70 different projects based on the two initial ones, giving the British Museum and UCL the autonomy to develop new projects on their own, as well as fixing them.

These outcomes have lead to master the technology and workflow thanks to the simplicity, good documentation and support from Scifabric's team.

Micropasts

The British Museum and University College London asked SciFabric to build a PyBossa-powered platform for researching the British Bronze Age.

We have helped to create high quality 3D models of bronze-age artefacts via a process known as photo-masking, and also to digitally transcribe the Museum’s huge database of record cards relating to these artefacts.

Visit Micropast

Cities at Night

The University of Madrid, in collaboration with NASA, leads one of Crowdcrafting’s most ambitious projects that aims to study light pollution emitted by cities at night.

By exploiting citizens’ local knowledge, researchers aim to catalogue and georeference a huge database of photographs taken by astronauts on board the International Space Station. This data will highlight which of the world’s cities are energy efficient and which are major light polluters.

Visit cities at night

Antimatter Alpha

CERN, the world’s leading particle physics laboratory and home of the Large Hadron Collider, has asked Scifabric and Crowdcrafting to help it research how antimatter interacts with gravity.

The AEgIS experiment requires volunteers to trace the tracks left behind by high energy particles travelling through a special gel called an emulsion – it’s as easy as joining the dots! Volunteer contributions will help researchers determine if antimatter falls up or down.

Visit Antimatter project
