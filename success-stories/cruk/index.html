Starting point

Trailblazer aimed to find wheter citizen science could be a

realiable method to accelerate cancer research

Citizen science holds true potential where researchers want to identify trends and patterns in large volumes of data.
This study investigates the efficacy of various training methods to improve accuracy on detecting several cancer type cells.
To do so, enhanced training and more feedback was provided and interactive tutorials and an easy-to-use interface were used to get people doing quality analysis, fast.

The project

1000 volunteers

Over 1000 volunteers without previous biomedical knowledge accessed Trailblazer, a web platform easy to use and with no special software required.

25 minutes of training

After a 25 minutes training, volunteers had to analyse 10 sample images. Their scores showed they could distinguish between cancer cells and healthy cells in tissue samples. Try the Trailblazer demo here.

Evaluated by specialists

When it came to analysing real tumor samples, citizen scientists’ scores were compared against consensus expert analysis to evaluate the accuracy.

And the result?

Trailblazer scored the highest accuracy reading of all Cancer

Research UK’s projects based on crowdsourcing proving that

citizen scientists could be truly valuable to researchers.

90% accuracy
Citizen scientists had learned to detect clinically significant features of cancer cells. Their score was compared against consensus expert analysis to evaluate the accuracy. Volunteers’ findings matched those of professional pathologists up to 90% of the time.
** Graphic shows example of Cancer Bladder analysis. Aggregating the analysis of 10 or more participants trained with both feedback and annotated examples, achieved accurate detection of cancer cells (Area Under Curve >0.90) for all samples tested.
